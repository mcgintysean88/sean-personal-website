# Sean McGinty — Personal Website Content Plan
### Phase 1 Source of Truth Document · Updated April 2026

---

## Overview

**What this site is:** A personal website. Not a business, not a sales pitch. A place that tells Sean's story, shows who he is professionally and personally, and sets the stage for a consulting practice launching later in 2026. The site should make someone feel like they genuinely know him — and want to stay in touch.

**What this site is not:** A services page. A portfolio of client work. A "hire me" pitch. Those things come later.

**The implicit message:** A marketer who builds a creative, well-crafted, AI-assisted personal website is showing — not telling — what they're capable of. The execution is the proof.

**Primary audience:** Future potential consulting clients, professional contacts, people who might refer business, and anyone who Googles Sean's name.

**Tone:** Warm and approachable. Direct. Confident without being corporate. A little wit goes a long way. Sounds like a smart, interesting person — not a LinkedIn profile.

**Personal content philosophy:** Enough personality to feel real. Details that reveal character, curiosity, and values. Not so much that it reads like a family blog. The personal layer is about *Sean* — his interests, his roots, the things that make him interesting beyond the job titles.

**Inspiration:**
- **jopecuro.com** — Scrolling interactions, bold animated typography; the *experience* signals sophistication
- **brentstirling.com** — Personality-first, no fluff, you know who this person is within seconds
- **jasonfaber.ca** — Clear professional structure, easy to understand what someone does and where they've been

#### Part 1: Professional story

I started my career at RKG — a boutique SEM firm in Charlottesville, Virginia — back when "data-driven marketing" was something people were figuring out in real time. I learned early that good marketing lives at the intersection of rigorous analysis and genuine human understanding. That combination became my thing.

From RKG I moved through a series of increasingly complex marketing challenges. At SnapCap, a fast-growing small business lender, I was the only marketer — which meant building everything from scratch. I owned acquisition, retention, and analytics, and helped grow the business to acquisition by LendingTree in 2017. That experience gave me something I've leaned on ever since: confidence that you can build a lot with very little, if you're focused.

LendingTree was a different kind of education. I ran product marketing for a six-product consumer portfolio including credit cards, personal loans, and business lending — and in Q1 2020, COVID hit while I was in the middle of it. Category revenues fell 80–87% in a single quarter. No playbook. I replaced conventional planning cycles with a daily performance trading cadence, making real-time budget and channel decisions on evolving data. We protected unit economics. We kept partner relationships intact. It was the hardest professional period I've had, and the most instructive.

Later at LendingTree I moved into Marketing Operations, where I architected the integration of 13 acquisitions into a unified MarTech and revenue stack. It was there that I became genuinely obsessed with the infrastructure underneath marketing — the data pipelines, the measurement architecture, the stack that either unlocks or limits what's possible.

That obsession followed me to TaxSlayer, where I joined as Head of Digital Marketing and was promoted to CMO following the company's acquisition by Marlin Equity Partners. Over three years, I led a team of 23 across brand, creative, performance, lifecycle, and product marketing — and rebuilt the marketing function from the ground up. We centralized a Snowflake-based Single Source of Truth, deployed multi-touch attribution and incrementality measurement, migrated lifecycle automation, and engineered a more sophisticated retention and LTV engine. The result: 11% revenue CAGR and 13% EBITDA CAGR on a $29M annual budget.

Throughout all of it, I've been paying close attention to AI — what it can actually do for marketing, where it creates real leverage, and where the hype outruns the reality. I'm in the early stages of building something around that. More on that soon.

#### Email obfuscation implementation notes

The email address should never appear in plain text in the HTML source. Two recommended approaches, in order of preference:

**Option 1 — Cloudflare Email Obfuscation (easiest, already available)**
Sean's domain is registered and proxied through Cloudflare. Cloudflare includes a free "Email Address Obfuscation" feature under Scrape Shield in the dashboard. When enabled, Cloudflare automatically rewrites any email addresses on the page using JavaScript obfuscation before serving it to visitors — humans see the address normally, bots see scrambled code. No code changes needed. Enable it in: Cloudflare Dashboard → seanmcginty.me → Scrape Shield → Email Address Obfuscation → On.

**Option 2 — JavaScript construction (fallback for static builds)**
Never write the email address as a plain string in HTML. Instead, construct it in JavaScript at runtime:
```html
<span id="email-link"></span>
<script>
  const u = 'smmcginty';
  const d = 'gmail.com';
  const el = document.getElementById('email-link');
  el.innerHTML = '<a href="mailto:' + u + '@' + d + '">' + u + '@' + d + '</a>';
</script>
```
This keeps the address out of the static HTML source entirely.

**Option 3 — Contact form via Formspree (no email displayed at all)**
Use a simple Formspree form (free tier) that accepts name + message and forwards to smmcginty@gmail.com. The email is never shown on the page at all. Most privacy-preserving, adds slight friction for visitors.

**Recommendation:** Enable Cloudflare obfuscation (Option 1) as the base layer, and use the JavaScript construction method (Option 2) as a belt-and-suspenders measure for any platform that doesn't proxy through Cloudflare during development.
